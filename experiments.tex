\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:exp_setup}

\input{tables/tab1_recovery_gradient}

\paragraph{Base model and evaluation.}
We evaluate on DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B~\citep{deepseek2025r1}, a 7B-parameter reasoning model built on the Qwen2.5 architecture~\citep{qwen2.5} with GQA~\citep{ainslie2023gqa} (Section~\ref{sec:prelim}).
Our primary benchmark is GSM8K~\citep{cobbe2021gsm8k} ($N{=}1{,}319$ grade-school math problems; exact-match accuracy).
The unpruned model achieves 90.83\% (greedy decoding).

\paragraph{Pruning methods.}
We compare four pruning criteria at 20\% structured sparsity, all removing the same number of MLP channel groups (200 of 784 across 28 layers, ${\sim}$1.45B parameters) with identical per-layer capacity floors (${\ge}$40\% retention per layer):
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{CKA-only}: structures ranked by functional overlap $F(h)$ alone (Eq.~\ref{eq:overlap}); the most redundant groups are removed regardless of gradient importance.
    \item \textbf{Random}: uniform random selection among eligible groups.  Uses TRL's default 512-token generation limit (vs.\ 1{,}024 for other methods), which may disadvantage its reward trajectory; we retain it as a conservative lower bound rather than a direct comparison point.
    \item \textbf{Standard}: Taylor-expansion importance $I(h)$ alone (Eq.~\ref{eq:importance}); the least important groups are removed, following LLM-Pruner~\citep{llmpruner2023}.
    \item \textbf{\rasp{}} ($\alpha{=}1.0$): the composite score $S(h) = I(h) \times (1 - F(h))^\alpha$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:score}), removing structures that are both low-importance and functionally redundant.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Recovery pipeline and hardware.}
Immediately after pruning (before any recovery), all four methods produce near-zero GSM8K accuracy ($<$2\%), as the pruned models fail to generate coherent reasoning chains or proper termination tokens.
All four methods then undergo matched two-stage recovery (Section~\ref{sec:recovery}): SFT warmup (2 epochs, 7{,}473 GSM8K examples, 37 min) followed by GRPO (lr $1{\times}10^{-6}$, $\beta{=}0.01$, $T{=}0.4$, 100 steps on 1{,}000 problems; full hyperparameters in Table~\ref{tab:hyperparams}, Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_training}).
All methods use \texttt{max\_completion\_length}{$=$}1{,}024 except Random (TRL default 512), making it a conservative comparison point.
We repeat GRPO with 3 seeds for CKA-only, Standard, and Random (\rasp{} at $\alpha{=}1.0$ uses a single seed); CKA-only achieves 64.39\% $\pm$ 0.19\,pp, Standard 59.84\% $\pm$ 0.38\,pp (Welch's $t$, $p{=}0.0009$, $d{=}11.1$; Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_seed}).
The ``Unpruned'' baseline in Table~\ref{tab:decoupling} uses the same SFT-warmup checkpoint; the 9.56\,pp gap from the pre-warmup 90.83\% reflects warmup on a reduced training set, not pruning loss.
All experiments run on 2$\times$ NVIDIA RTX PRO 6000 GPUs (94\,GB VRAM each).


\subsection{Recovery Capacity Spectrum}
\label{sec:recovery_gradient}

Under GRPO training, the four pruning criteria produce a clear recovery capacity spectrum: CKA-only $\gg$ Random $\gg$ Standard $>$ \rasp{} at $\alpha{=}1.0$ (Table~\ref{tab:recovery_gradient}, Figure~\ref{fig:training_dynamics}).
The pruning criterion, rather than training configuration, determines whether the model learns, stagnates, or exhibits reward collapse.

Among these configurations, only the diversity-maximizing criterion (CKA-only) sustains active learning: peak reward 0.354 with $f_0$ as low as 0.125 (Figure~\ref{fig:training_dynamics}b).
Random, under a shorter generation limit, stagnates (reward 0.14--0.19, min $f_0 = 0.300$); Standard exhibits borderline collapse ($f_0 \geq 0.50$, peak reward 0.081); \rasp{} at $\alpha{=}1.0$ exhibits complete reward collapse ($f_0 \geq 0.675$, reward declining to 0.010, with near-zero KL divergence indicating minimal policy movement; Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_kl}).
The dose-response analysis (\S\ref{sec:sparsity_cliff}) confirms CKA weighting as the critical variable: increasing $\alpha$ from 1.0 to 2.0 shifts \rasp{} from reward collapse to peak recovery (66.11\% vs.\ 64.39\% for CKA-only on GSM8K).


\subsection{Reward-Accuracy Decoupling}
\label{sec:decoupling}

\input{tables/tab2_decoupling}

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/paper/fig_sft_grpo_scatter.pdf}
    \caption{SFT vs.\ GRPO accuracy across all pruning criteria and sparsity levels on GSM8K ($N{=}1{,}319$). Points near the $y{=}x$ line indicate reward-accuracy decoupling: despite 4.4$\times$ peak reward gaps (Figure~\ref{fig:training_dynamics}a), GRPO yields minimal accuracy change ($|\Delta| \leq 1$\,pp for all fine-grained methods at 20--25\%). \rasp{} points correspond to $\alpha{=}1.0$.}
    \label{fig:sft_grpo_scatter}
\end{figure}

Despite the 4.4$\times$ peak reward gap, GRPO produces minimal accuracy change (Figure~\ref{fig:sft_grpo_scatter}): all fine-grained methods at 20\% sparsity differ by $|\Delta| < 1$\,pp (CKA-only $+$0.48, Standard $+$0.55, Random $-$0.53, \rasp{} at $\alpha{=}1.0$ $-$0.61\,pp; Table~\ref{tab:decoupling}).
We term this \emph{reward-accuracy decoupling} (observed at 20--25\% fine-grained MLP sparsity): reward trajectory reflects the model's capacity to learn from RL signal, not downstream accuracy.
At 20\% sparsity, the four fine-grained criteria show mean $|\Delta| {=} 0.54$\,pp (max $0.61$\,pp); across the full $\alpha$ dose-response spectrum, $|\Delta|$ remains below $1.14$\,pp.

The degree of decoupling varies with sparsity: at 15\%, GRPO amplifies the CKA-only advantage (SFT gap 5.69\,pp $\to$ GRPO gap 7.20\,pp); at 20--25\%, $|\Delta| < 1$\,pp without exception across all four fine-grained criteria (Tables~\ref{tab:decoupling},~\ref{tab:cross_sparsity}; Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_cross_sparsity}); dose-response $\alpha$ variations remain below $1.14$\,pp (Table~\ref{tab:alpha}; MATH-500 within noise, Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_math500}).

Two One-Sided Tests (TOST) equivalence testing ($\varepsilon{=}2$\,pp, chosen as ${\approx}4\times$ cross-seed $\sigma$; $N{=}1{,}319$ paired examples) confirms statistical equivalence for 7/8 comparisons ($p{<}0.05$; margin justification and per-pair results in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_tost}), robust across seeds (0.37\,pp range; Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_multiseed}) and extended training (300 steps; \S\ref{sec:extended_analysis}).
The endpoints of the spectrum generalize beyond GSM8K: on MATH-500~\citep{hendrycks2021math}, CKA-only and Standard both rank above \rasp{} and Random in SFT accuracy (27.4\%, 25.6\% vs.\ 21.6\%, 16.6\%), though the CKA-only--Standard gap narrows (1.8\,pp vs.\ 4.62\,pp on GSM8K; Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_math500}).
This compression is consistent with two factors: MATH-500's higher intrinsic difficulty---the unpruned model achieves only 53.0\% (vs.\ 90.83\% on GSM8K)---and generation-length truncation at the 2{,}048-token evaluation limit, which affects over half of dense-model responses (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_math500}).
On MBPP~\citep{austin2021mbpp} all fine-grained GRPO $|\Delta| < 2$\,pp, though not statistically significant at this sample size (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_mbpp}).
\label{sec:ood}
ShortGPT~\citep{shortgpt2024} depth pruning is the sole exception: its GRPO delta is task-dependent ($-$1.36\,pp on GSM8K, $+$4.01\,pp on MBPP; Tables~\ref{tab:decoupling},~\ref{tab:mbpp}), unlike fine-grained methods ($|\Delta| \leq 1.8$\,pp).
This asymmetry may reflect differing sensitivity to sequential depth: multi-step mathematical reasoning (GSM8K) relies on extended chain-of-thought computation across layers, which layer removal directly disrupts, whereas the function-level code generation tasks in MBPP depend more on local pattern matching and may benefit from reduced overthinking in shorter networks.


\subsection{Additional Results}
\label{sec:additional_results}

\paragraph{Post-SFT accuracy does not predict GRPO outcome.}
\label{sec:sft_gradient}
Under SFT alone the ordering is CKA-only $>$ \rasp{} at $\alpha{=}1.0$ $>$ Standard $>$ Random, yet the recovery capacity spectrum (Table~\ref{tab:recovery_gradient}) reverses the \rasp{} at $\alpha{=}1.0$/Standard ordering---Standard exhibits higher peak reward than \rasp{} despite lower SFT accuracy, indicating that the spectrum tracks structural properties rather than post-SFT accuracy.

\paragraph{Dose-response and sparsity limits.}
\label{sec:sparsity_cliff}
The CKA coefficient $\alpha$ continuously modulates recovery capacity: on GSM8K, recovery follows an inverted-U across $\alpha \in \{0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 3.0, 4.0\}$, peaking at $\alpha{=}2.0$ (66.11\%; Figure~\ref{fig:dose_response}, Table~\ref{tab:alpha}) with $|\Delta| \leq 1.14$\,pp; \rasp{} at $\alpha{=}1.0$ exhibits reward collapse while \rasp{} at $\alpha{=}4.0$ recovers to 62.77\% (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_alpha}).
The pattern generalizes to MATH-500 ($\alpha{=}1.0$ worst at 23.0\%; higher-$\alpha$ cluster at 27.2--27.6\%; Table~\ref{tab:math500}).
All methods collapse at 30\% sparsity (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_sparsity_cliff}); pruning reduces parameters by 19\% and memory by ${\sim}$28\% (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_inference}).


\subsection{Extended Analysis}
\label{sec:extended_analysis}

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/paper/fig4_cka_heatmap.pdf}
    \caption{Layer-wise CKA similarity between dense and pruned representations (28 layers). CKA-only diverges most from the dense model ($\mu{=}0.62$) yet achieves the highest recovery; Standard ($\mu{=}0.90$), Random ($\mu{=}0.92$), and \rasp{} at $\alpha{=}1.0$ ($\mu{=}0.88$) preserve dense fidelity but exhibit reward collapse under RL.}
    \label{fig:cka_heatmap}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Representational diversity.}
CKA similarity between dense and pruned representations (Figure~\ref{fig:cka_heatmap}) shows that CKA-only diverges most from the dense model ($\mu{=}0.62$), while Standard ($\mu{=}0.90$), Random ($\mu{=}0.92$), and \rasp{} at $\alpha{=}1.0$ ($\mu{=}0.88$) cluster near 1.0---preserving dense fidelity retains redundant representations at the expense of diversity.
CKA-only concentrates pruning in early layers (L0--9, 72.5\% of removed groups; Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_overlap},~\ref{sec:appendix_depth_first}), yielding 2.89$\times$ mean gradient magnitude versus Standard (55/56 layer groups $p{<}0.05$; Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_gradient}).
On MATH-500, CKA-only truncates only 22\% of completions (mean 655 tokens) versus Standard's 44\% (mean 1{,}128 tokens), yet achieves equal or higher accuracy (Table~\ref{tab:math500}).

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/paper/fig5_extended_training.pdf}
    \caption{Extended GRPO training (300 steps, 3$\times$ budget). (a)~Standard reward: zero net gain (0.054$\to$0.056); CKA-only sustains a 5.5$\times$ gap. (b)~Standard $f_0$ remains in the collapse zone ($>$0.5); CKA-only stays below 0.30.}
    \label{fig:extended_training}

\paragraph{Extended training does not resolve collapse.}
Extending GRPO from 100 to 300 steps (Figure~\ref{fig:extended_training}; Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_extended}), Standard shows no net improvement (reward 0.054--0.058, $f_0 > 0.5$ throughout), while CKA-only sustains a 5.5$\times$ gap (0.308 vs 0.056 at step 300), suggesting the gap is structural rather than a matter of training budget.

\paragraph{Cross-scale and cross-model generalization.}
Preliminary evidence at 1.5B scale (Table~\ref{tab:cross_scale}; Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_cross_scale}) preserves the three-method ordering.
On Qwen2.5-Math-7B, a different model within the Qwen2.5 family (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_cross_arch}), CKA-only recovers from 1.0\% post-prune to 51.50\% vs.\ Standard's 21.00\%.
